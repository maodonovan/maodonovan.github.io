Chapter 1 Going to court

1.1 Q10

Respondents were asked how many times they had been in court over the past 12 months. Just over one-third indicated that their current appearance had been the only one. A far greater proportion, 63 percent, indicated that they had been to court at least twice in the past year. The data suggest that Mthatha and King Williams Town (the courts most distant from respondents) had the lowest ‘return rate’. Respondents from those courts indicated that they had, on average, been to court 2.9 and 2.6 times over the past 12 months. By contrast respondents from Oudtshoorn had been to court and average of 4.3 times in the last year.

Differences in the return rate varies also according to the reason why people were in court. Those attending to obtain a court order had been to court more often than respondents in other categories (3.8 times). Those returning to court for the lowest number of times were witnesses (2.9), By comparison respondents accused of an offence had been to court significantly more often (3.6). The differences in frequency between witness and accused corresponds to accused not being called for initial hearings where trial dates etc are set.

1.2 Q15c. Before I was in the court room (the last time), my opinion of the court system was positive.

Respondents were asked how their opinion of the court system had changed since they were last in a SA court. Almost three-quarters 77.2 % said they had a positive opinion of the court system the previous time they were in court.

After their latest experience this proportion dropped slightly to 72.1 %. However this may be attributed to sample error rather than any decline in the quality of the court experience. Only One-in-five respondents did not think that the court experience was positive (or neutral).

Somewhat surprisingly those who had the most positive opinion of the courts were those accused of an offence. 80.7 % said their experience was positive. With those at court in support of a family member or friend being the most negative with one-third (32.4 %) disagreeing with the statement that the experience was positive.

1.6 Q11. How many different cases did you go to court for?

Almost all groups had an average of two cases in the last year. Those going to court to access public records had the highest number of cases on average.

It was not assumed that people returning top court in the last year were appearing for the same case. Respondents were asked how many different cases they were in court for during the period. Respondents, on average, said they went to court for two cases (1.98). Those accused of an offence indicated they were in court for an average of 2.1 cases - i.e. indicating the probability that they had been accused of at least one other offence. Accused were thus had the second highest number of cases after those accessing records.

The large number of cases respondents were involved with does not fully account for frequency with which respondents were in court over the past year. The balance has to be attributed to cases being postponed in court or with (for those accessing documents and orders) the documentation not being available.

1.7 Find out where to go: difficulty

The vast majority (78.1 %) of respondents found it easy to find out where to go in the court. Among court users those individuals accused of an offence had the greatest difficulty (29%). Thus while most people did not have difficulty finding out where to go a significant proportion did have some difficulty. The problem was greatest (28.5 %) among those more likely to be penalised for being late (or not arriving at all) i.e the accused.

1.15 Treatment of respondents

Respondents were asked if they felt that were treated with respect by various court officials. Small minorities felt they were not respectfully treated by magistrates (7.3 %), police (13 %), court clerk (7.9 %), prosecutors (9.1 %), interpreters (6.2 %), their own lawyer (7.8 %). Once again there was little variation between respondent categories. In all instances those accused of an offence held opinions similar to the rest of those at court.
