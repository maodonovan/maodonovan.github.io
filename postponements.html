Chapter 5 Postponements

5.1 I was able to do what was need in reasonable time

Respondents were, generally, of the opinion that they were able to accomplish what they needed in a reasonable amount of time. 80 % agreed with the statement that “I was able to do what was needed in a reasonable time”. Although there was some variation in responses in terms of the reason why the respondent was in court these were not significant statistically. This suggests that frustration with the court system may not stem from time processes taking overly long.

5.2 “Case delayed unnecessarily”

While most respondents (55%) disagreed with the statement that the case they were in court for had been unnecessarily delayed. This indicates that, only by a slim margin, most respondents were happy with delays (or accepted the reason for the delay as legitimate). A significant minority 39.2 agreed that that their case had indeed been delayed unnecessarily. The sentiment was relatively consistent among respondents regardless of the reason why they were in court. Court delays thus constitute the greatest source of negative sentiment with the judicial system.

5.3 “Case delayed unnecessarily” & travel distance

It was postulated that long travel distance would make respondents more sensitive to delays and the issue was explored after deriving distances from the court and the respondents origin. This proved not to be the case. While 40.8% of respondents who travelled less than 20km felt the delay was unnecessary a smaller proportion 34.3%) of those travelling 20km or more thought the delay unnecessary. This suggests that frustrations with delays are less associated with the level of personal inconvenience than they are with the “legitimacy’ of the delay.

5.4 Reasons for delaying/postponing court cases

Respondents were asked why the case in question was postponed. The most frequently cited reason was that the court file was missing or not available. This was cited by almost one-fifth (18.8%) of those called to court. The next most frequently cited reason was the non availability of witnesses (15%), legal representation not being available (11%) and the magistrate not being available or the accused asking for a postponement (9.3% each). In less than one percent of cases was the delay due to a court room not being available.

“Missing dockets” were cited more often than any other reason for cases being postponed (16%) of citations. This reason was closely followed by ‘other’ reasons (15%).

The issue of “Missing dockets” was particularly pronounced in Oudtshoorn where it was cited by half the respondents.

5.5 Impact of delays on frequency in court

The respondents were grouped into two. The first were those that disagreed with the statement that “the case was delayed unnecessarily”. The second were those that agreed with the statement. Their replies were correlated to the question as to how many times they had been to court in the past 12 months. Those that agreed with the statement had been an average of 4.2 times. Those that disagreed with the statement had only been to court an average of 3.5 times. In other words those that felt the cases had been delayed unnecessarily had been subject to more frequent delays/postponements.
